Hands: a thoughtful static site generator

and written in Rust (just kidding)

It's launch day, baby! This essay marks the first third time that Bob has launched his blog.

Instead of using a tool like Wordpress, Jekyll, or Medium, all the HTML and CSS on this website is written by hand (no JavaScript). For any aspiring writer who is at least somewhat technical, this essay describes the benefits of working this way and explains how you can get started. Even if you have decades of experience in web development, constraining yourself to handwrite HTML and CSS will help you focus on improving your writing.

Why write a website by hand?

Performance

HTML has become the assembly language of websites. There are many static site generators that can automate your blogging process and optimize your website for the modern web, but add bloat to simple use cases. If you ever want to run your website on an embedded device with low power and high performance, then you should write your HTML by hand. The right tool for the job, or whatever.

Education

Especially for beginner web developers, writing HTML by hand will teach you how websites work under the hood. Not only this, these website fundamentals have a long half-life, and these concepts will carry forward to any frontend web framework you may use in the future: ReactJS, Angular, VueJS, Svelte, and so on. As empires rise and fall, HTML will remain as the foundation of web development.

Focus

Tinkering with new features is the downfall of software engineers who try to start their own blogs. Your technical curiosity will distract you from the core process of writing content. Without constraints, you fall into the trap of ending each day by adding 120kB to your website without a single new paragraph. Not good.

You should know that writing is a creative endeavour, and constraints are good for creativity. By limiting the surface area of what you are allowed to work on, you force yourself to focus on generating ideas and actually write. The writing process is difficult and, like anything else, it takes intentional practice to see improvement. Luckily, it can be made easier if you add constraints to how fancy your website is.

At first, it will be painful. There's so much pre-work to do, such as SEO, asset optimization, analytics, and automation. You may ask, "how do we know if we're engaging our audience?" Well, to be frank, you won't and you shouldn't. The goal is to start writing and finish something. And then start writing a second thing and finish that one too. The whole point is to improve your language skills, not your programming skills.
