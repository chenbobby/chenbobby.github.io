Disagreeable values

Anyone in charge of making decisions should have disagreeable values, whether it's your opinion about high-level ideas or nitty gritty details.

One of the most important lessons that my father taught me is that all decisions have trade-offs. Growing up, he would repeatedly say that "you have to take the whole package. The good and the bad". Then, when I went off to study computer science in college, this idea came up in every class: time vs. space complexity; CAP theorem; read- vs. write-optimized; every choice in your system design or algorithm will involve a sacrifice.

Ever since I went from studying to working, I've been learning more about strategy and team culture. This is partially due to a small interest in building my own company in the future, but also due in large part to making sure that the job I have is aligned with my own core values. Innovation vs. stability; broad vs. niche; flat vs. hierarchical; each way you decide to build your product or run your company will have strengths and weaknesses. You have to take the whole package.

In both engineering and business, however, there are cases where people don't acknowledge the trade-offs that they're making. Many projects in tech are fueled by hype, only to come down crashing when scaling up reveals performance or security issues. Similarly, company websites often have fluffy language about culture that tell me nothing about what is and isn't a priority. It's extremely frustrating, because it's impossible to know which values you hold if you don't know which values you are willing to give up. When you are trying to explain what you are, it is doubly useful to state what you are not.

Agreeable values are useless

Core values such as "be hardworking" and "be kind" are agreeable but useless. The reason that they are agreeable is because no one will want to argue against you with the opposite side: "be lazy" and "be rude". It's almost like preemptive strawmanning, if there's such a thing.

The reason that they are useless is because they don't acknowledge the trade-offs. And the trade-offs matter a lot. If a CEO preaches "be hardworking" as a company value, an employee will have no idea how that applies to their job. How hard are you supposed to be working exactly? Should you keep working hard even if you get no recognition for my efforts?

If you ask such questions for your company's core values, or even your own personal values, then you will see that any virtue can become a vice in the extreme. I would even argue that being extreme (or being moderate) has a tradeoff to be acknowledged. An interesting thing happens when you scrutinize these values: they start to become disagreeable.

There's an example of some supposed strengths that can turn into nasty weaknesses:

  • "Proven" becomes "legacy".
  • "General purpose" becomes "unspecialized".
  • "Feature-rich" becomes "steep learning curve".
  • "Highly configurable" becomes "complex".
  • "Trusted by many" becomes "unable to innovate".
  • And so on.

Disagreeable values are specific

Someone who wishes to play politics doesn't necessarily want a majority to agree with them. Rather, they want a majority to not disagree with them. And the best way to do that is to be as vague as possible about what you stand for. With a vague expression, you can slip and slide your way around opposition, dropping a "well that's not what I meant" every now and then.

On the other hand, once you get specific, you will start to show tradeoffs. You will inevitably show weaknesses in your argument when you are more clear and concrete. Now people can really start poking holes, either in your starting assumptions and/or your line of reasoning. These critics will fall into one of two camps.

One group of critics will disagree with you because they themselves hold a set of opposing but also disagreeable values, in which case you are to them as they are to you.

The other group of critics will also disagree with you, but they differ from the first camp in that they hold very generic and agreeable values, sitting on their righteous pedestal of vagueness, only to become inconsistent and unreliable hypocrites when a crisis demands a real decision to be made. Watch out for those in this camp, and definitely don't be convinced by them and become a follower.

These are just my own thoughts on the matter. I hope that you disagree.
